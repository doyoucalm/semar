# Strength (VIII)

## Image
A woman in white robes, crowned with flowers, gently closes the jaws of a lion. A lemniscate (∞) floats above her head — the same symbol the Magician wears. Her touch on the lion is light; the lion's tail is between his legs. A green hill rises behind.

## Core Meaning
Power exercised through patience. Strength is not the overcoming of the animal but the alliance with it. The lion is not bound or muzzled; he has been met. Read as fortitude that comes from inside, the kind that does not need to declare itself, and the slow work of relationship between the conscious figure and the instinctive force.

## Upright
The card's earlier traditional name was *Fortitude* — but the imagery is not about iron will. The woman's gesture is one of communion. She has spent time with the lion; she has not feared him; she has not killed him. The lemniscate above her — borrowed from the Magician — signals that this is mastery of the same order as his, but turned inward, toward what the body and the unconscious carry. In a reading, Strength points to inner work: the courage to feel without flinching, the patience to remain when there is no quick resolution.

## Reversed
The alliance broken. Reversed, Strength may indicate the lion taking over — instinct, fear, or appetite running the show — or the opposite excess: the figure trying to dominate the lion by force, and being torn for the trouble. In another reading the reversal marks self-doubt, the loss of belief in one's own capacity to meet what arrives.

## In a Reading
Beside the Chariot, the card distinguishes the warrior's drive from the saint's patience. Beside the Hermit, inner work in two modes: communion versus retreat. In a question about a difficult emotion, Strength is the answer that says "stay with it." In a question about another person, the card asks whether they are being met with patience or with force.

## Keywords
fortitude, gentleness, inner power, patience, the lion within, courage, communion with instinct, doubt (reversed), force (reversed)

## Sources
- Arthur Edward Waite, *The Pictorial Key to the Tarot* (Rider, 1910) — Waite swapped Strength to position VIII and Justice to XI, against the Marseille tradition.
- Rachel Pollack, *Seventy-Eight Degrees of Wisdom* (Aquarian Press, 1980) — Strength as the soul's relationship with its own animal nature.
- Aleister Crowley, *The Book of Thoth* (OTO, 1944) — the Thoth deck names this card *Lust* and shifts the gender entirely; useful for contrast.
