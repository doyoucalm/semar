# The Hierophant (V)

## Image
A robed figure sits between two grey stone pillars, hand raised in benediction. Crossed keys lie at his feet. Two tonsured monks kneel before him. He wears a triple crown and holds a triple cross staff. The pillars are bare of ornament; the temple is austere.

## Core Meaning
Tradition. The Hierophant is the institution, the lineage, the teaching that comes from outside and asks to be received before it can be questioned. He is religion in the etymological sense — *religare*, to bind again. Read as the teacher, the established way, the ritual that holds a community together over generations. Where the Magician's craft is individual, the Hierophant's authority is transmitted.

## Upright
The two kneeling monks are not the Hierophant's equals; he is the link in a chain longer than himself. He represents the value of the established path — the apprenticeship to a master, the marriage performed by a priest, the rite of passage that the community gives shape to. There is humility in the upright form: the willingness to receive what one did not invent. In study, he is the canon; in life, the institutional framework that confers belonging.

## Reversed
Tradition turned brittle, or tradition rightly refused. Reversed, the Hierophant may indicate the institution that has forgotten its purpose — orthodoxy without spirit, ceremony without meaning, the priest who serves the office rather than the call. On the other side, the same reversal blesses the heretic, the one who walks away from a teaching that no longer fits. The crossed keys can open or refuse to open.

## In a Reading
Beside the Lovers, the Hierophant marks the difference between the institutional marriage and the personal vow. Beside the Hermit, established versus solitary paths to the same knowing. In a question about study, he favours the master and the school over autodidactic improvisation; in a question about belief, he favours examination of inherited form before its rejection.

## Keywords
tradition, lineage, institution, the teacher, orthodoxy, ritual, conformity, belonging, dogma (reversed), rebellion (reversed)

## Sources
- Arthur Edward Waite, *The Pictorial Key to the Tarot* (Rider, 1910) — the keys and triple cross are Waite's deliberate ecclesiastical layering.
- Rachel Pollack, *Seventy-Eight Degrees of Wisdom* (Aquarian Press, 1980) — Hierophant as the link in a chain of transmission.
- Benebell Wen, *Holistic Tarot* (North Atlantic, 2015) — modern reading of the card in secular institutional contexts.
