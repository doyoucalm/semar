# The Devil (XV)

## Image
A horned, bat-winged figure crouches on a black pedestal. Two naked figures — clearly the Lovers, returned — stand chained to the pedestal. The chains around their necks are loose enough to remove, but they have not removed them. The Devil holds a torch downward; his right hand makes an inverted blessing. A pentagram is inscribed on his forehead, point-down.

## Core Meaning
Bondage that is not actually locked. The Devil is the card of compulsion, addiction, materialism in its enslaving form, and every situation where the chain holding the figure is the figure's own grip on it. Read as attachment that has become identity, shadow that has been refused recognition, and the long patient work that will be required to look at it directly.

## Upright
The card's most important detail is the looseness of the chains. The figures could lift them over their heads and walk away. They do not — because the chain has become familiar, or because the gain of the bondage is being clung to alongside its cost. The figures returning the Lovers' imagery is deliberate: the Devil is the union turned into entrapment, the choice that has frozen into a compulsion. Tradition reads the card as material attachment, addiction, the relationship one cannot leave for reasons that are not what they appear to be. The card is associated with Capricorn — the goat-horned figure on its peak.

## Reversed
The chain noticed. Reversed, the Devil often signals the beginning of release — the recognition of the compulsion as compulsion, which is the first move toward setting it down. On the other side, the same reversal can show the deepening of the bondage: the chain now hidden, the addiction now denied. Both readings turn on awareness.

## In a Reading
Beside the Lovers, the difference between chosen union and compulsive attachment is the whole question. Beside Temperance, the slow patient work of integration is the way out of the chain. In a relationship reading, the Devil is the bond one cannot honestly call love; in a habit reading, it is the loop one has run for years.

## Keywords
bondage, compulsion, addiction, attachment, shadow, materialism, the unsought chain, Capricorn, awareness beginning (reversed), denial (reversed)

## Sources
- Arthur Edward Waite, *The Pictorial Key to the Tarot* (Rider, 1910) — the visual rhyme with the Lovers is Waite's deliberate counterpoint.
- Rachel Pollack, *Seventy-Eight Degrees of Wisdom* (Aquarian Press, 1980) — the Devil as the shadow, the unintegrated material self.
- Aleister Crowley, *The Book of Thoth* (OTO, 1944).
