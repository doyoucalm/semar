# Suit of Wands — Fire

## Element & Sphere
**Fire.** The suit of will, drive, spirit, and the creative impulse before it has been given form. Where Cups concern the heart's water and Pentacles the earth's substance, Wands are the spark that wants to burn — the urge to begin, to make, to assert, to travel, to compete. In life-domain terms: enterprise, vocation in its driving aspect, passion, conviction, and the experience of being animated by something larger than the personal.

## Astrological Association
The fire signs — Aries, Leo, Sagittarius. Aries especially carries the suit's youngest and most direct quality; Leo its radiance and pride; Sagittarius its expansion and questing.

## Visual Signature
In the Rider-Waite-Smith deck, each wand is depicted as a budding wooden staff — leaves still growing on it. The suit is alive; the wood has not been cured into furniture. This is the constant note of the Wands suit: vitality that has not yet been domesticated.

## Pip Progression
The Ace is the spark itself, offered like a torch from a cloud. The two through ten walk a recognisable arc — initiative (2), expansion (3), foundation (4), conflict and competition (5), victory (6), defence (7), speed (8), endurance (9), and the over-burdened completion of a fire-cycle (10).

## Court Archetypes
- **Page of Wands** — the messenger of fire, the young enthusiast, news of a venture beginning.
- **Knight of Wands** — the warrior of fire, headlong and charismatic, sometimes reckless.
- **Queen of Wands** — fire mastered with warmth, the matriarch of passion, generous and self-possessed.
- **King of Wands** — fire mastered with vision, the leader who casts the long line of intent, capable of inspiring others to act.

## Reading Note
Wands answer questions of motivation, vocation, energy, and direction of will. In health they ask about vitality; in relationship, about passion and shared purpose; in work, about whether the questioner is doing what genuinely animates them.

## Sources
- Arthur Edward Waite, *The Pictorial Key to the Tarot* (Rider, 1910).
- Rachel Pollack, *Seventy-Eight Degrees of Wisdom* (Aquarian Press, 1980) — long treatment of the four suits as elemental quarters of experience.
- Joan Bunning, *Learning the Tarot* (Weiser, 1998) — practical pip-by-pip approach to the suit.
