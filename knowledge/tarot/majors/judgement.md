# Judgement (XX)

## Image
A winged angel — Gabriel — sounds a trumpet from a cloud, a red cross on a white banner streaming from it. Below, the dead rise from grey coffins floating in water: a man, a woman, a child, arms raised. Distant mountains line the horizon. The scene is bright, almost dawn-light.

## Core Meaning
The call to which one finally answers. Judgement is the card of vocation in the older sense — *vocare*, to be called — and of the public reckoning where the long, private work shows up in plain daylight. Read as awakening, rebirth, the moment the questioner recognises what they have been called to and turns toward it. The trumpet is not a threat; it is a summons.

## Upright
The rising figures are not being judged as good or bad in some final ledger; they are being raised, and the rising itself is the event. The card carries an air of inevitability: what is being summoned has been forming for a long time underground. Tradition reads the upright card as the awakening — to vocation, to truth about oneself, to the next chapter — and as the public recognition of what the questioner already knew privately. There is forgiveness here too: the old self set down without shame, the new self received without strain.

## Reversed
The call ignored. Reversed, Judgement may indicate the summons one has heard and refused to answer — the vocation deferred, the truth-about-self not acknowledged, the reckoning postponed. On the other side, the same reversal points to harsh self-judgement, the refusal of forgiveness toward oneself or another, the inability to leave the coffin.

## In a Reading
Just before the World (the final card of the journey), Judgement marks the second-to-last station — the awakening that makes the completion possible. Beside Death, the difference between letting go of an old form and rising into a new one. In a career reading, the call to a vocation; in an inner reading, the moment the long private work becomes a public commitment.

## Keywords
awakening, vocation, rebirth, the call, public reckoning, forgiveness, the trumpet, ignored call (reversed), harsh self-judgement (reversed)

## Sources
- Arthur Edward Waite, *The Pictorial Key to the Tarot* (Rider, 1910).
- Rachel Pollack, *Seventy-Eight Degrees of Wisdom* (Aquarian Press, 1980) — Judgement as the answering of the call rather than a courtroom verdict.
- Aleister Crowley, *The Book of Thoth* (OTO, 1944) — the Thoth deck renames this card *Aeon* and shifts the imagery to a more cosmic register.
