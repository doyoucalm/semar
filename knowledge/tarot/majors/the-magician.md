# The Magician (I)

## Image
A figure stands at a table on which the four suit emblems are laid out: a wand, a cup, a sword, a pentacle. One hand points upward with a white wand of its own; the other points down to the earth. A lemniscate (∞) crowns the head, and a serpent-belt circles the waist. Lilies and roses bloom in the foreground — white above, red below.

## Core Meaning
Will become form. The Magician is the moment intention finds its instruments. Every tool the questioner needs is already on the table; the question is whether they can be picked up. Read as agency, conscious action, focused attention. The upward and downward hands trace the old hermetic axiom "as above, so below" — heaven and earth joined by the body of the practitioner.

## Upright
This is the card of the artisan, the maker, the one who knows the trade. The four emblems represent the four elements (fire, water, air, earth) and the four worlds of action available to the figure. The Magician's power is not derived from secret knowledge; it is derived from showing up at the table with all four instruments ready. There is concentration here, the gathered quality of someone whose attention is not split. In a chart of stations, this is where raw potential first takes definite shape.

## Reversed
The Magician's craft becomes manipulation — power for power's sake. Reversed, the figure may indicate trickery (the literal carnival "magician" the card is sometimes named after in older decks), self-deception, or talent left unused. The tools are still on the table, but the practitioner has stepped away. In another reading the reversal can mean blocked action: the will is there, the instruments are not yet trusted.

## In a Reading
Near the start of a layout, the Magician answers "do I have what I need?" with yes. In the present position, it pushes the questioner toward the work itself rather than further preparation. Beside the Fool, the journey moves from possibility to first craft. Beside the High Priestess, the conscious actor and the silent knower stand together — both forms of knowing.

## Keywords
will, focus, manifestation, craft, agency, the four tools, action, concentration, manipulation (reversed), unused talent (reversed)

## Sources
- Arthur Edward Waite, *The Pictorial Key to the Tarot* (Rider, 1910).
- Rachel Pollack, *Seventy-Eight Degrees of Wisdom* (Aquarian Press, 1980) — connects the Magician to Hermes-Mercury and to the moment of becoming conscious of one's own resources.
- Robert Place, *The Tarot: History, Symbolism, and Divination* (Tarcher, 2005) — for the card's evolution from the medieval *bateleur* (juggler) to the modern magus.
